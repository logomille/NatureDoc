Introduction

Gender Studies 5120, Gender & Nature, has been a very unique experience- which is actually what I was expecting and hoping for. I feel as if I have a grasp on the goals of the course, however I continuously struggle to dig deep. I have never truly connected with any sort of plant. I can appreciate them, admire them, fight for them, but I have never felt as if we could communicate with each other. I never would have considered them admiring me in the same way that I admire them. I have always felt a deeper connection with animals, and I was eager to observe a fauna for my documentary, however, this assignment wouldn’t be fulfilling it’s full potential if I didn’t push myself to connect with something I’ve never attempted to connect with before.

I moved into my new apartment in July of 2022. I fell in love with the location because of how close it was to campus, and the surroundings are abundant with various species of wild growing plant life. We live in the basement, so the backyard isn’t really ours, but it still makes me so pleased to see that most of it is beautiful clover, and we get plenty of shade from the surrounding trees.

There are large, green tendrils that clutch our chain link fence, and when I first met them I found them to be annoying as they make it hard to open the fence, and they drape over the other side so that you have to duck to get by. I never realized they were grape vines until the bright green orbs growing on them caught my eye.

Overarching Theme: Sexual Identity

When I first noticed the grapes I experienced excitement. How cool was it that we had produce growing on our fence? I was eager to pluck them from the vines and try them for myself, but I stopped myself because I was worried that my landlord wouldn’t appreciate that. Even if they were intentionally there, or simply wild, it is her property and not mine to take from. But it didn’t cross my mind, until I started my documentary, to consider what the grapes would have wanted.

I imagine the grapes' perspective: I am desired, I am wanted by someone. This person has the power to take me, and I don’t have the power to stop them, but they stop themselves because they fear another person who they believe has power over me.

You may already be drawing some obvious connections to this experience and how our society has viewed and treated women, especially in older generations when women were usually handed over for marriage by their fathers. We may still see this today in the outdated tradition of “getting permission” from a woman's father before proposing, however it also shows up in more subtle ways, like when people refer to their partners as “theirs”. This isn’t necessarily problematic on its own, however it can normalize possessive behavior, which is especially harmful in the context of stereotypical masculine/feminine roles in relationships- as well as in viewing femininity from a masculine perspective in general.

I am disappointed that I have perpetuated these masculine stereotypes by having a possessive, and entitled, mindset. I wanted to anthropomorphize these grapes to better understand them, but from the beginning I had attached a sort of feminine label to them because of their beauty and appeal, but doing so helped me to recognize my own toxic conceptions and stereotypes of femininity. I’m grateful for this learning experience, and I plan to correct my masculine presumptions of femininity as they arise.

Overarching Theme: Racial Identity

In these images, the leaves seem to pop out more than the grapes themselves, so I’ve decided to include them in my documentation. Grape leaves can be used in a number of dishes, the most notable generally originating in the Middle East. Many different cultures use grape leaves to compliment their culinary endeavors, however research has also supported medicinal uses as well. [1]

So, why is it that we first ask the uses of a plant when we discover them? There are many ways to look at this question… First off, maybe a plant's use isn’t the question that you first jump to, and the reason I did may be part of my own culture. One with a colonizer mindset would probably always first consider the uses of a thing (i.e. plants, animals, land), whereas another culture may still view these things as useful, but not in a way that involves them being used. The key difference is how both view their relationship with these subjects, because these views influence their interactions. Someone who has gratitude with nature will recognize the benefits from nature, and they will practice reciprocity to give back. A colonizer may recognize the benefits from nature and choose to maintain it with the intention that they will receive more- they view nature as an investment rather than a gift.

There is nothing wrong with learning the functions of nature, but it’s important that I don’t overlook any selfishness and/or entitlement that may develop, or has already existed, from my colonizer upbringing. I plan on viewing nature as a mutual relationship rather than just a resource.

Overarching Theme: Gender Identity

I caught these pictures in the early morning. When I look at these images they appear cold to me, but for each of these days the air was warm; perhaps I have a strong association with the cold and morning light.

I often categorize things by colors and/or temperature depending on the associations I have with it. Some are obvious, like my childhood home is warm and a soft gold color, because homes are usually warm and comforting, and gold happens to be a favored color of mine. Warm things are not always positive, just as cold things aren’t always negative. The morning may be cold to me, even when it physically isn’t, but I have a fondness when it comes to seeing the morning light split into rays as it rises behind our mountain peaks.

Throughout history, communities and cultures have developed their own associations with grapes. In some areas, grapes are considered a delicacy, likely because there are high costs associated with transporting fresh grapes since they perish quite fast. In other areas, specifically areas in which grapes are truly native, they grow rampant in the climate they are used to, so they are considered common, but the wine they make becomes a staple in many of their native countries. The Greek god Dionysus is credited for being the first to create wine through the cultivation of grapes, and he’s had many labels attached to him because of this. He is regarded as a god of fertility, ecstasy, joy, and dance, however he has also been associated with debauchery, corruption, and bad luck for the same reasons. [2] It’s interesting what type of symbolism grapes have depending on the perspective that views them.

Just as wine may symbolize openness and fun, or alternatively, irresponsibility and “immodesty” (depending on who you ask), queerness comes with its own symbolism. Queerness, in relation to the LGBTQ+ community, may symbolize freedom, truth, and genuinity for those who identify with it, whereas it may be seen as entirely opposite by someone who struggles to understand the community.

Being in the LGBTQ+ community means my labels may hold weight depending on the perspectives that are viewing them. They mean something to me, but I can’t help it if they spark an entirely different message in someone else- the only thing I can offer is my perspective. There are times when others may hold the same labels as me but don’t quite fit the definition that I’ve set upon that label, or they may fit the definition of my label but choose to identify outside of my group, but I can’t help that the vines of their identity have delicately intertwined with their nature and unique experiences in a way that grows separately from mine.

Identity Deconstruction

“The most important clue to identify the grape variety is the leaf,” according to Per Karlsson, an author of BKWineMagazine. I was surprised to find out that there are many things that a professional “grape leaf identifier” will look at to determine the grape variety, including, but certainly not limited to, the size, shape, color, and texture of the leaf. They also take a look at the pattern and distribution of the nerves, which may have extremely subtle differences between various leaf types. [3] Overall, identifying what grapes you’re looking at feels nearly impossible as an amateur, and I feel like the same can be said for anyone that is exploring new, unfamiliar parts of their identity.

There’s a lot of pressure that comes with identity exploration. At certain points in your life you’re expected to be comfortable and self-assured with various aspects of your identity, even though everyone’s journey is so different and exploration may come a lot easier to some than it does to others.

Understanding ourselves is a crucial part of our existence, however there seems to be so much baggage that comes from attaching labels to ourselves. When we adopt labels it becomes easier to find and connect with others with the same label, however it’s important to remember that the ways in which we experience these shared identities are unique to us. These communities, while comforting, also make it easier to other those outside of it, and then we start to forget all of the similarities we have with everyone around us. I believe it helps me to strengthen my connection with other people and nature when I consider us all existing as a single, natural organism. Sometimes, we need to deconstruct our identities to see the full picture.
